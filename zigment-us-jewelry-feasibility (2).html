2. Right to Win Assessment
What makes Zigment credible here? An honest look.
The answer is not "nothing" — but it's also not "everything." You have genuine technical differentiation. You lack market-specific proof.

✓ Conversation Graph is real differentiation

No US competitor offers persistent cross-channel context that follows a customer from Instagram DM → SMS → web → store. Tidio, Gorgias, LivePerson, ManyChat — all are single-channel or stateless. This isn't a feature gap; it's an architectural advantage that's hard to replicate.

✓ Proven ROAS in same vertical

BlueStone — a real jewelry brand with a real $600 AOV — at 10–20× ROAS. Not a demo. Not a simulation. Not a different vertical. Same product category, same customer psychology. The use case translates even if the channel differs.

✓ TCPA compliance already solved

Give.org deployment on toll-free SMS proves you can operate in the US regulatory environment. Most India-born AI companies stumble here. You've already cleared the hurdle.

✓ Multi-channel from Day 1

Instagram DMs, SMS, web chat, WhatsApp, email — all live, all carrying context. US jewelry needs this because there's no single dominant messaging channel (unlike India's WhatsApp). Your omnichannel capability is a US-market necessity, not a nice-to-have.

✗ No US jewelry case study

BlueStone is India + WhatsApp. US buyers, agencies, and investors will ask: "Does this work with American consumers on Instagram and SMS?" You can't answer that yet. This is the single biggest credibility gap.

✗ No US sales infrastructure

An SF investor doing 6–8 hrs/week and founder visits 3–4x/year is not a sales team. For enterprise/mid-market B2B SaaS in the US, you need someone who can do lunches, QBRs, and "let me walk you through the dashboard" calls in US time zones.

✗ No US brand recognition

Zigment has zero presence in the US conversational AI discourse — no G2 reviews, no Gartner/Forrester mention, no case study on any US marketing blog. You're starting from zero awareness in a market flooded with AI vendors.

✗ Pricing is unvalidated in US

$0.30/conversation works in India. US pricing of $1–2/conversation is hypothesized, not tested. If US jewelry brands are already paying $0.50/conversation for Tidio or getting "free" chatbots from Shopify apps, your pricing power is unknown.

Right-to-Win Verdict: Conditional.

You have a genuine technical moat (Conversation Graph) and relevant vertical proof (BlueStone). This puts you ahead of "nothing." But technical superiority without local proof and local presence doesn't close enterprise deals in the US. The right to win exists but must be earned in the first 6 months with 2–3 US pilot wins.

4. Required Capabilities & Partnerships You Don't Have
What you need to build, buy, or borrow

US Agency Partner (Critical)

You need 1–2 performance marketing agencies with active jewelry clients to co-sell. They provide: client access, trust transfer, ad account management, and ongoing QBR presence.

Fix: Formalize a partner program with rev-share. Target Logical Position, Common Thread Collective, or a boutique jewelry-specialist agency. 60-day timeline.

Shopify Plus Integration (Important)

80%+ of US D2C jewelry brands run on Shopify Plus. Without a native Shopify app or deep integration (catalog sync, order data, checkout attribution), you can't prove ROAS end-to-end.

Fix: Build a Shopify Plus integration. 4–8 week engineering sprint. List on Shopify App Store for discovery.

Instagram DM API Expertise (Important)

Instagram DMs are the most direct CTWA equivalent in the US. Meta's IG Messaging API has specific approval requirements, rate limits, and UX constraints. You need battle-tested implementation.

Fix: If not already built, prioritize IG DM channel to production-grade quality. Test with 1 US brand before scaling. 4–6 week effort.

US-Localized Brand Voice Training (Important)

American jewelry consumers have different conversational norms than Indian consumers. Tone, humor, urgency, and objection patterns differ. Your AI needs to sound like a Nordstrom associate, not a helpful chatbot.

Fix: Work with first US pilot to co-develop brand voice library. Record real in-store sales conversations as training data. 30-day effort per brand.

G2/Gartner/Review Presence (Nice-to-have)

US B2B buyers validate via G2, Capterra, and analyst reports. Zero reviews = zero credibility in procurement-led deals. Less critical for founder-led and agency-referred deals.

Fix: Seed 5–10 G2 reviews from existing clients (India OK). Apply for Gartner Cool Vendor consideration. 90-day effort.

US Payment/Billing Infrastructure (Operational)

US clients expect USD invoicing, net-30 terms, and integration with their AP systems. Wire transfers from India create friction and compliance questions.

Fix: US entity (if not already), Stripe billing, and standard SaaS contract templates. One-time setup, 30 days.

6. Kill Criteria — When to Stop
These signals should make you exit.
You asked for this, and it's the most valuable section. Markets are littered with startups that kept investing past the point of evidence. Define your exit signals now, while you're thinking clearly.
🔴

Kill Signal 1: Pilot ROAS below 3× after 60 days

If your first US pilot — with full support, optimized brand voice, and co-invested ad spend — can't produce at least 3× ROAS within 60 days, the BlueStone results don't transfer to US channels. The fundamental thesis (conversational selling works for US jewelry) is invalidated.

Trigger: ROAS < 3× at Day 60 of first US pilot → Pause all US investment. Diagnose root cause. If not fixable in 30 days, exit.
🔴

Kill Signal 2: Zero paying clients after 6 months

If after 6 months of active effort — with SF investor, founder visits, agency outreach, and 1–2 pilots — you have zero clients converted from pilot to paid, the market isn't rejecting your technology; it's rejecting your GTM. And fixing GTM in a market you don't live in is extremely expensive.

Trigger: 0 paying US clients at Month 6 → Halt spend. Evaluate whether the problem is product-market fit, pricing, or GTM. If it's GTM, consider acqui-hiring a US-based co-founder or team before proceeding.
🔴

Kill Signal 3: US pricing compresses below $0.50/conversation

If US brands anchor to Tidio pricing ($0.50/conversation for Lyro) or expect "free" chatbot-tier pricing, your unit economics don't work. At $0.50/conv with US cost structure, you need 3–4× the volume per client to break even — and jewelry doesn't have that volume. The India-pricing-×-3-for-US model collapses.

Trigger: 3+ prospects or agencies push back on pricing above $0.75/conv → Re-evaluate value proposition and pricing model. Consider per-conversion or revenue-share pricing instead.
🟡

Caution Signal: Agency partnerships stall beyond Month 9

If no agency has signed a formal partnership and deployed to at least 1 client by Month 9, the channel strategy isn't working. This doesn't kill the market — but it means you're running a direct sales motion only, which is 3× slower and more expensive. Re-assess headcount and burn.

Trigger: 0 active agency deployments at Month 9 → Shift budget from agency GTM to direct outbound. Consider hiring a 2nd US AE focused on direct brand sales.
🟡

Caution Signal: Cumulative US burn exceeds $200K with <$50K revenue

This is a financial guardrail. If you've spent $200K and generated less than $50K, the ROI trajectory is dangerous for a startup. Not an automatic kill — but requires a board-level conversation about whether continued investment is justified by leading indicators (pipeline, pilot results, LOIs).

Trigger: Review at the $200K burn mark. If pipeline value < 3× burn → escalate to board.
Final Recommendation
Proceed — but with staged commitment

Verdict: Conditional Go. Not a blank check.

This is not a bad idea. The market is structurally attractive, your differentiation is real, and the BlueStone proof is more than most startups enter new markets with. The jewelry vertical has a genuine, quantifiable pain point (sub-1% conversion, 80% cart abandonment) that your Conversation Graph is architecturally positioned to solve.

But it is a $300K bet that needs staged gates. Do not invest the full amount upfront. Structure it in three phases:

Phase 1 (Months 1–3, ~$75K): Get one US pilot live. This is the "minimum viable entry." If the pilot fails, you've spent $75K to learn the market isn't ready — not $300K. Your SF investor should be sourcing the first pilot NOW, before you commit additional capital.

Phase 2 (Months 4–6, ~$75K): Publish the case study. Sign one agency. If both happen, you've validated GTM. If neither happens, invoke Kill Signal 2 and re-assess.

Phase 3 (Months 7–12, ~$150K): Scale. Hire the US AE. Invest in conferences and content. This phase only unlocks if Phase 1 and 2 produce evidence.

The single most important thing you can do in the next 30 days: Get one US jewelry brand — any size, even small — to run a 30-day pilot on Instagram DMs or SMS. Everything else is contingent on that result. If your SF investor can't source one pilot introduction in 30 days, that itself is a signal about your US GTM readiness.
